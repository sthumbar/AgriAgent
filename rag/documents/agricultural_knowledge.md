# Agricultural Knowledge Base

## Tomato Diseases

### Early Blight (Alternaria solani)

Early Blight is a fungal disease caused by *Alternaria solani* that affects tomato, potato, and other Solanaceae crops. It is one of the most common foliar diseases worldwide.

**Symptoms:** Dark brown to black lesions with concentric rings (target-board appearance) appear on lower leaves first. Lesions are surrounded by a yellow halo. Severely infected leaves turn yellow and drop prematurely.

**Cause:** The fungus overwinters in infected plant debris and soil. Spores spread via wind, rain splash, and contaminated tools. Warm temperatures (24–29°C) and high humidity (>90%) favour disease development.

**Treatment:**
- Apply chlorothalonil (1.5–2.0 kg/ha) or mancozeb (2.0 g/litre) at first sign of symptoms
- Rotate with azoxystrobin or propiconazole fungicides to prevent resistance
- Remove and burn infected leaves immediately
- Apply copper-based fungicide as a protective measure

**Fertilizer for recovery:** Apply balanced NPK 19-19-19 at 50 kg/ha every 3 weeks. Supplement with calcium nitrate (15-0-0 + 26% Ca) to strengthen cell walls and reduce susceptibility.

**Irrigation:** Water at the base using drip irrigation. Avoid overhead irrigation. Water twice weekly in the morning (25mm/session). Reduce frequency during rainy periods.

**Organic alternatives:** Neem oil (5ml/litre) applied weekly as foliar spray; Trichoderma harzianum soil drench (2g/litre); copper Bordeaux mixture (1%); baking soda solution (10g/litre).

**Prevention:** Maintain 45–60 cm spacing between plants for airflow. Practice 3-year crop rotation with non-solanaceous crops. Use certified disease-free seeds. Mulch to prevent soil splash.

### Late Blight (Phytophthora infestans)

Late Blight is caused by the oomycete pathogen *Phytophthora infestans*. It caused the Irish Potato Famine (1845–1849) and remains a major threat to potato and tomato production globally.

**Symptoms:** Water-soaked, irregular dark lesions on leaves and stems, often with white sporulation on the underside. Fruit develops brown, firm rot. Entire plants can collapse within days.

**Cause:** Cool, wet conditions (10–20°C with high humidity) are optimal for spread. Spreads rapidly via wind-borne sporangia.

**Treatment:**
- Apply metalaxyl-M + mancozeb (Ridomil Gold MZ) at 2.5 g/litre preventively and curatively
- Cymoxanil + mancozeb is effective for established infections
- Apply every 5–7 days during high-risk periods

**Fertilizer:** High-potassium fertilizer (0-0-60) at 100 kg/ha to strengthen plant defenses. Avoid excessive nitrogen which promotes soft growth susceptible to infection.

**Irrigation:** Reduce irrigation frequency during cool, humid periods. Use drip irrigation only. Never overhead irrigate.

**Organic alternatives:** Copper hydroxide (50% WP, 3g/litre); Bacillus subtilis (QST 713 strain) biofungicide; bordeaux mixture.

### Powdery Mildew

Powdery Mildew is caused by *Leveillula taurica* on tomatoes. It is an obligate biotrophic fungus.

**Symptoms:** White powdery patches on upper leaf surfaces. Infected leaves turn yellow and eventually drop. Unlike most fungi, it thrives in warm, dry conditions with moderate humidity.

**Treatment:** Sulfur-based fungicides (80% wettable sulfur at 3g/litre); potassium bicarbonate; myclobutanil.

**Fertilizer:** Reduce nitrogen to avoid excessive vegetative growth. Apply silicon (SiO₂) foliar spray to strengthen leaf cuticle.

## Wheat Diseases

### Wheat Rust (Puccinia spp.)

Wheat rust includes stem rust (*P. graminis*), yellow/stripe rust (*P. striiformis*), and leaf rust (*P. triticina*). All are serious threats to global wheat production.

**Symptoms (Stem Rust):** Reddish-brown pustules on stems and leaves. Severely infected plants lodge and produce shrivelled grain.

**Symptoms (Stripe Rust):** Yellow/orange stripes of pustules running along leaf veins. Occurs in cooler conditions (5–15°C).

**Treatment:**
- Propiconazole (Tilt 25 EC) at 500 ml/ha
- Tebuconazole at 750 ml/ha
- Apply at flag leaf stage or at first sign of pustules

**Fertilizer:** Balanced NPK with emphasis on potassium (K) for disease resistance. Apply 120 kg N/ha in split doses. Avoid luxury nitrogen application.

**Irrigation:** Maintain soil moisture at field capacity. Avoid overhead irrigation that promotes rust sporulation. Furrow or drip irrigation preferred.

---

### Fusarium Head Blight (Scab)

Caused by *Fusarium graminearum*. Produces mycotoxins (deoxynivalenol/DON) that make grain unsafe for consumption.

**Symptoms:** Pink-orange sporulation on florets. Bleached spikelets during grain fill. Shrivelled, chalky grain (tombstones).

**Treatment:** Prothioconazole (Proline 480 SC) at flowering. Metconazole. Application timing at 50% flowering is critical.

**Fertilizer:** Balanced NPK. Avoid excessive nitrogen which promotes dense canopy and humidity favourable for infection.

## Rice Diseases

### Rice Blast (Pyricularia oryzae)

The most destructive rice disease worldwide, affecting leaves, nodes, and panicles.

**Symptoms (Leaf Blast):** Diamond-shaped lesions with grey centres and brown margins. Severe infections kill leaves and reduce tillering.

**Symptoms (Neck/Panicle Blast):** Rotting at the neck just below the panicle, causing the head to fall over (lodging).

**Treatment:**
- Tricyclazole (75% WP) at 500 g/ha for leaf blast
- Isoprothiolane (40% EC) at 1.5 litre/ha
- Apply within 24 hours of symptom appearance

**Fertilizer:** Reduce nitrogen application during blast-prone periods. Apply silica (SiO₂) as it strengthens cell walls. Split nitrogen in 3 applications rather than one large dose.

**Irrigation:** Maintain 5cm water depth in paddy fields. Intermittent flooding reduces humidity at canopy level.

**Organic alternatives:** Pseudomonas fluorescens biocontrol (spray 0.5%); Trichoderma viride (5g/litre soil drench); silicon-rich amendments (rice hull ash).

---

### Bacterial Leaf Blight (Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae)

A major bacterial disease of rice, particularly in Asia.

**Symptoms:** Water-soaked to yellowish stripe along leaf margins, progressing to entire leaf. Bacterial ooze visible on cut stems in cross-section.

**Treatment:** Copper-based bactericides (copper oxychloride 50% WP at 3g/litre). No effective systemic bactericide available — prevention is key.

**Fertilizer:** Balanced NPK with reduced nitrogen. Potassium at 60 kg/ha improves resistance.

## Maize / Corn Diseases

### Northern Corn Leaf Blight (NCLB)

Caused by *Exserohilum turcicum*. Common in humid maize-growing regions.

**Symptoms:** Long, cigar-shaped, tan to greyish-green lesions (5–15 cm) on leaves. Severe infection can defoliate plants before tasselling.

**Treatment:** Azoxystrobin + propiconazole (Quilt Xcel) at VT/R1 growth stages. Pyraclostrobin + metconazole.

**Fertilizer:** Adequate nitrogen (180–200 kg N/ha in split applications). Potassium at 80–100 kg/ha for disease resistance. Zinc supplementation (25 kg ZnSO₄/ha) if deficient.

**Irrigation:** Furrow or drip irrigation. Avoid overhead irrigation. Irrigate to maintain consistent soil moisture especially during V8–VT stages.

---

### Corn Smut (Ustilago maydis)

A fungal disease causing tumour-like galls on any above-ground plant part.

**Symptoms:** Large, irregular, spongy galls covered with a whitish membrane that ruptures to release black spores. Common on ears, tassels, and stems.

**Treatment:** No effective chemical control once established. Remove and destroy galls before they rupture.

**Fertilizer:** Balanced NPK. Avoid excessive nitrogen which promotes lush growth susceptible to smut entry through wounds.

---

## Cotton Diseases

### Cotton Root Rot (Phymatotrichopsis omnivora)

A soilborne fungal disease primarily in arid and semi-arid regions.

**Symptoms:** Sudden wilting of the entire plant while leaves remain attached. Bronze-coloured leaves. Root surface shows tan strands of fungal mycelium.

**Treatment:** Fumigation with metam sodium in high-value situations. Lime application to raise soil pH >7.5.

**Fertilizer:** Potassium-rich fertilizer (KCl or K₂SO₄). Avoid acid-forming fertilizers. Apply organic matter (compost, manure) to improve soil biology.

**Organic alternatives:** Trichoderma harzianum soil drench at transplanting; compost teas; beneficial mycorrhizal inoculants.

## General Fertilizer Guidelines

### NPK Ratios for Common Crops

| Crop | N (kg/ha) | P₂O₅ (kg/ha) | K₂O (kg/ha) | Key Notes |
|------|-----------|--------------|-------------|-----------|
| Tomato | 150–200 | 80–100 | 150–200 | Split N in 4 doses; Ca essential |
| Wheat | 120–150 | 60–80 | 60–80 | Topdress N at tillering |
| Rice | 100–120 | 40–60 | 40–60 | K aids blast resistance |
| Maize | 180–220 | 80–100 | 80–100 | Zn deficiency common |
| Cotton | 100–120 | 50–60 | 80–100 | B important for fruiting |
| Potato | 150–200 | 100–120 | 200–250 | K critical for tuber quality |

### Micronutrient Supplementation

- **Zinc (ZnSO₄):** 25 kg/ha basal for maize, rice, wheat. Foliar 0.5% spray.
- **Iron (FeSO₄):** Foliar spray 0.5% for iron chlorosis in calcareous soils.
- **Boron (Borax):** 1–2 kg/ha for flowering crops (cotton, sunflower, canola).
- **Calcium (Gypsum):** 500–1000 kg/ha for peanuts, tomatoes, brassicas.
- **Magnesium (Epsom Salt):** Foliar 2% spray for chlorosis.

## Irrigation Best Practices

### Water Requirements by Crop (mm/season)

| Crop | Total Water Need | Critical Stages |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Tomato | 400–600 mm | Flowering, fruit set |
| Wheat | 450–650 mm | Tillering, grain fill |
| Rice (paddy) | 900–1200 mm | All stages |
| Maize | 500–800 mm | Tasselling, grain fill |
| Cotton | 700–1300 mm | Square formation, boll fill |

### Drip Irrigation Advantages
- Reduces fungal disease by keeping foliage dry
- Improves fertilizer use efficiency (fertigation)
- Reduces water consumption by 30–50% vs. flood irrigation
- Minimises weed germination

### Signs of Water Stress
- **Under-irrigation:** Leaf rolling, wilting in afternoons, stunted growth, purple discolouration
- **Over-irrigation:** Yellow lower leaves, root rot, fungal disease outbreaks, waterlogging

## Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Principles

1. **Monitor regularly:** Scout fields at least twice per week
2. **Economic thresholds:** Treat only when pest/disease population exceeds threshold
3. **Biological control first:** Encourage natural predators; use biofungicides/bioinsecticides
4. **Cultural practices:** Crop rotation, sanitation, resistant varieties
5. **Chemical control last resort:** Use selective, least-toxic options; rotate modes of action
6. **Resistance management:** Never rely on a single active ingredient for more than 2 seasons

## Organic and Biological Treatments

### Neem Oil
- **Active ingredient:** Azadirachtin
- **Dosage:** 5 ml neem oil + 2 ml emulsifier per litre water
- **Application:** Foliar spray every 7–10 days
- **Controls:** Aphids, whitefly, thrips, mites, powdery mildew
- **Withdrawal period:** None (safe for harvest)

### Trichoderma harzianum
- **Type:** Beneficial soil fungus (biocontrol agent)
- **Application:** Seed treatment (4g/kg seed); soil drench (2g/litre)
- **Controls:** Pythium, Rhizoctonia, Fusarium root rots
- **Benefits:** Promotes root growth, improves drought tolerance

### Bacillus subtilis (QST 713)
- **Brand name:** Serenade (various)
- **Application:** Foliar spray 2–4 litre/ha
- **Controls:** Botrytis, Sclerotinia, powdery mildew, early blight
- **OMRI listed:** Yes (certified for organic production)

### Pseudomonas fluorescens
- **Application:** Seed treatment or soil drench
- **Controls:** Damping off, crown rot, some foliar pathogens
- **Additional benefit:** Promotes plant growth through PGPR activity

### Bordeaux Mixture
- **Composition:** Copper sulfate + hydrated lime (1:1 in water)
- **Controls:** Downy mildew, late blight, bacterial diseases, leaf spots
- **Caution:** Can be phytotoxic in hot weather; do not apply within 3 days of sulfur

## Soil Health and Crop Rotation

### Recommended Crop Rotation Sequences

**Rotation 1 (Solanaceous crops):**
Tomato/Potato → Maize/Wheat → Legume (Soybean/Groundnut) → Tomato/Potato

**Rotation 2 (Cereals):**
Wheat → Rice → Legume → Wheat

**Rotation 3 (Mixed):**
Cotton → Wheat → Sorghum → Cotton

### Benefits of Crop Rotation
- Breaks pest and disease cycles
- Improves soil organic matter
- Reduces fertilizer requirements (nitrogen fixation by legumes)
- Controls weeds through varied herbicide programs

### Soil Testing Recommendations
- Test soil every 2–3 years for pH, N, P, K, and micronutrients
- Optimal pH ranges: Most crops 6.0–7.0; Blueberry 4.5–5.5; Potato 5.0–6.0
- Adjust pH with agricultural lime (to raise) or elemental sulfur (to lower)
